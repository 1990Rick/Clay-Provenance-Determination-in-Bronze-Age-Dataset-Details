Introduction to the Dataset
This documentation provides a comprehensive reference guide for all 20 analytical sheets and their respective column definitions within the Excel dataset entitled 'Reassessing Clay Provenance Determination in Bronze Age Anatolian Ceramics Through the Combined Application of Rare Earth Element Fingerprinting, Strontium Isotope Ratios, and Petrographic Thin-Section Analysis: Toward a Multi-Proxy Methodological Framework.' The dataset is designed to serve as a scientifically rigorous dummy/reference dataset for archaeometric research methodology development, training, and demonstration purposes.
The dataset encompasses 20 worksheets, each containing 5,001 populated cells (one merged title row, one header row, and 249 data rows across 20 columns), totalling 100,020 cells. The data covers 249 ceramic specimens and clay reference samples from 20 Bronze Age Anatolian archaeological sites, assigned to five geochemically distinct provenance groups corresponding to major geological zones of Anatolia: the Central Anatolian Crystalline Complex (Group-A), Western Aegean Zone (Group-B), Pontide Zone (Group-C), Tauride Platform (Group-D), and Eastern Anatolia (Group-E).
The analytical framework integrates rare earth element (REE) concentrations determined by ICP-MS, strontium isotope ratios measured by TIMS and MC-ICP-MS, and petrographic thin-section modal analysis, together with supporting major element XRF geochemistry, grain size analysis, firing temperature determinations, and a suite of multivariate statistical analyses (PCA, LDA, cluster analysis). All geochemical values are generated within empirically established ranges for Bronze Age Anatolian ceramic assemblages as reported in the peer-reviewed archaeometric literature.
 
Sheet 01: Sample_Registry

The Sample Registry constitutes the foundational index of the entire dataset, serving as the master catalogue from which all analytical sheets derive their sample identifiers. Each row represents a single ceramic sherd or clay reference specimen subjected to one or more of the analytical protocols documented in subsequent sheets. The registry records provenance metadata, contextual archaeological information, and administrative tracking fields that ensure complete traceability from excavation context to geochemical laboratory result. This sheet is the primary key table: the Sample_ID field functions as the unique foreign key referenced across all 20 analytical sheets.

Entries include qualitative observations such as surface weathering, organic temper traces, secondary burning, calcareous crust formation, or recommendations for reanalysis, drawn from a standardised pool of commonly encountered conditions in ceramic archaeometry.

 
Sheet 02: XRF_Major_Elements

Major element oxide geochemistry, determined by X-ray fluorescence spectrometry (XRF) and verified by ICP-OES, constitutes one of the principal analytical pillars of ceramic provenance research. The concentrations of the ten major oxide components — expressed as weight percentages and normalised to a volatile-free basis via Loss on Ignition — reflect the bulk mineralogical composition of the ceramic fabric and its parent clay. Derived geochemical indices such as the Chemical Index of Alteration (CIA), Index of Compositional Variability (ICV), and various oxide ratios provide discriminant parameters sensitive to source geology, weathering history, and post-depositional alteration. Group-specific major element signatures differ systematically between the five defined provenance groups, enabling primary screening of ceramic origins prior to the application of more resource-intensive trace element and isotopic methods.

Column Definitions
Column Name	Data Type	Description & Scientific Significance
Sample_ID	Text	Unique specimen identifier as defined in Sheet 01 (Sample_Registry), functioning as the primary key for cross-sheet data linkage. All values in this column must correspond exactly to identifiers present in the master registry.
SiO2_wt%	Decimal	Silicon dioxide content expressed as weight percent of the dried and fused specimen. SiO2 reflects the quartz and silicate mineral content of the ceramic fabric. Values range from approximately 54–69 wt% across the dataset, with Western Aegean (Group-B) samples exhibiting the highest silica contents and Eastern Anatolian (Group-E) samples the lowest, consistent with differences in source geology.
TiO2_wt%	Decimal	Titanium dioxide content in weight percent. TiO2 is largely immobile during sedimentary and diagenetic processes and is incorporated into the ceramic fabric through heavy mineral phases (ilmenite, rutile, titanite). Its concentration, typically 0.65–1.05 wt% in this dataset, varies systematically with provenance group and serves as a robust discriminant when normalised against Al2O3.
Al2O3_wt%	Decimal	Aluminium oxide content in weight percent, reflecting the clay mineral fraction of the ceramic fabric. Al2O3 is among the most stable major element constituents under firing conditions up to ~1100°C and serves as the reference denominator for numerous provenance-sensitive ratios (SiO2/Al2O3, Fe2O3/Al2O3, Al2O3/TiO2). Values range from approximately 13.5 to 18.5 wt%.
Fe2O3_wt%	Decimal	Total iron content expressed as Fe2O3 equivalent in weight percent. Iron occurs in ceramic fabrics principally as haematite, magnetite, and iron-bearing clay minerals. Its concentration (5.0–8.5 wt% in this dataset) influences fabric colour and is sensitive to firing atmosphere. The Fe2O3/Al2O3 ratio provides a useful provenance discriminant independent of dilution effects.
MnO_wt%	Decimal	Manganese oxide content in weight percent, present at trace-to-minor levels (0.07–0.14 wt%) in ceramic fabrics. Mn may be enriched relative to geological backgrounds through diagenetic processes or deliberate addition of manganese-rich temper. Elevated MnO values may indicate post-depositional manganese enrichment from groundwater.
MgO_wt%	Decimal	Magnesium oxide content in weight percent, reflecting the presence of magnesian clay minerals (chlorite, palygorskite), dolomite, or mafic minerals in the ceramic fabric. MgO contents are distinctly elevated in Tauride group (Group-D) ceramics (4.5 ± 1.0 wt%) relative to other groups, consistent with calcareous source clays from the carbonate-dominated Tauride Platform.
CaO_wt%	Decimal	Calcium oxide content in weight percent, reflecting calcite, dolomite, and plagioclase feldspar contributions to the ceramic fabric. CaO shows the greatest inter-group variability in this dataset (range ~2–11 wt%), with Tauride group samples characteristically calcium-rich. Elevated CaO (>8 wt%) indicates calcareous fabric compositions that influence firing behaviour and secondary mineralogy.
Na2O_wt%	Decimal	Sodium oxide content in weight percent, primarily derived from plagioclase feldspar and natrosilite phases in the ceramic fabric. Na2O is susceptible to post-depositional leaching and therefore exhibits greater analytical variance than other major elements. The K2O/Na2O ratio is more robust than individual concentrations for provenance discrimination.
K2O_wt%	Decimal	Potassium oxide content in weight percent, primarily reflecting K-feldspar and illitic clay mineral contributions. K2O is relatively resistant to post-depositional alteration compared to Na2O. Pontide group (Group-C) ceramics exhibit the highest mean K2O values (3.6 ± 0.7 wt%), consistent with illite-dominated clay sources in the Pontide zone.
P2O5_wt%	Decimal	Phosphorus pentoxide content in weight percent. P2O5 occurs at minor concentrations (0.12–0.25 wt%) in ceramic fabrics and may reflect apatite in the source clay, organic phosphorus from burial environments, or post-depositional phosphate enrichment from organic decomposition. Anomalously elevated values may indicate contamination or organic temper.
LOI_wt%	Decimal	Loss on Ignition, expressed as weight percent, representing the mass loss upon heating to 1000°C. LOI reflects volatile components including structural water in clay minerals, carbonate decomposition, and oxidation of organic matter. Tauride group samples exhibit the highest LOI values (6.5 ± 1.8 wt%) consistent with their calcite-rich fabric compositions.
Total_wt%	Decimal	The sum of all ten major oxide components and LOI, expressed as weight percent. Analytical totals within the range 98.5–101.5 wt% are flagged as PASS; values outside this range are flagged as CHECK, indicating potential analytical error, hygroscopic moisture uptake, or unaccounted volatile phases. This field serves as a primary quality-control indicator for major element analyses.
SiO2/Al2O3	Decimal	The molar ratio of silicon dioxide to aluminium oxide, providing a measure of the relative silica enrichment of the ceramic fabric independent of absolute concentration. This ratio discriminates between quartz-rich sandy fabrics (high ratio) and clay-dominated compositions (low ratio) and correlates with source rock type (e.g., granite versus pelite).
K2O/Na2O	Decimal	The weight ratio of potassium oxide to sodium oxide, serving as an indicator of feldspar type and clay mineral assemblage in the source material. Values >2 indicate dominance of K-feldspar and illite over plagioclase and smectite. This ratio is more robust than individual alkali concentrations for provenance discrimination given Na2O's susceptibility to leaching.
Fe2O3/Al2O3	Decimal	The weight ratio of total iron oxide to aluminium oxide, providing a matrix-independent measure of iron enrichment that correlates with the iron-bearing mineral content of the source clay. Systematically elevated values characterise Eastern Anatolian (Group-E) ceramics and may reflect enrichment in ophiolite-derived iron phases in eastern Anatolian clay sources.
CIA	Decimal	The Chemical Index of Alteration, calculated as Al2O3/(Al2O3+CaO+Na2O+K2O)×100 (molecular proportions). CIA quantifies the degree of chemical weathering of the source material, with values approaching 100 indicating intense weathering and near-complete conversion of feldspars to clay minerals. CIA values in this dataset range from approximately 50–90, reflecting the diversity of clay source weathering histories across Anatolia.
ICV	Decimal	The Index of Compositional Variability, calculated as (Fe2O3+K2O+Na2O+CaO+MgO+TiO2)/Al2O3. ICV discriminates first-cycle sediments derived directly from igneous or metamorphic sources (ICV>1) from mature, recycled sediments and highly weathered residual clays (ICV<1). ICV values complement CIA in characterising the geological provenance and weathering maturity of the ceramic source clays.
Provenance_Group	Text	The assigned geochemical provenance group for the specimen, as determined through multivariate statistical analysis of the complete major element dataset. Five groups are defined: Group-A (Central-CACC), Group-B (Western-Aegean), Group-C (Pontide), Group-D (Tauride), and Group-E (Eastern-Anatolia), each corresponding to a distinct geological zone with characteristic major element compositions.
QC_Flag	Text	A binary quality-control flag indicating whether the analytical total (Total_wt%) falls within acceptable bounds. PASS indicates a total between 98.5 and 101.5 wt%, consistent with acceptable XRF analytical precision. CHECK indicates values outside this range and prompts review of the analytical procedure, sample preparation, or potential contamination.

 
Sheet 03: REE_Concentrations_ppm

Rare Earth Element (REE) concentrations, determined by quadrupole ICP-MS following lithium metaborate fusion and acid dissolution, represent the most sensitive geochemical fingerprinting tool available for ceramic clay provenance determination. The fourteen lanthanide elements from lanthanum (La, Z=57) to lutetium (Lu, Z=71), together with yttrium where measured, exhibit characteristic relative abundance patterns — the REE fingerprint — that are controlled by source rock type, weathering history, and sedimentary fractionation processes. Because REEs are effectively immobile during low-temperature diagenesis and are not substantially redistributed during ceramic firing at temperatures below ~1100°C, their patterns in ceramics directly reflect those of the parent clay. Systematic differences in absolute REE concentrations and in calculated parameters such as ΣREE, LREE/HREE ratios, and cerium and europium anomalies define the five provenance groups with high discriminatory power.

Column Definitions
Column Name	Data Type	Description & Scientific Significance
Sample_ID	Text	Unique specimen identifier as defined in the master Sample_Registry (Sheet 01), linking each REE analysis to the corresponding archaeological and contextual metadata.
La_ppm	Decimal	Lanthanum concentration in parts per million (µg/g). As the lightest and most abundant REE in geological materials, La provides the anchor point for chondrite-normalised REE patterns. Group-specific La concentrations range from ~22 ppm (Group-D, Tauride) to ~65 ppm (Group-E, Eastern Anatolia), reflecting differences in the REE-hosting mineral assemblage of source clays.
Ce_ppm	Decimal	Cerium concentration in ppm. Ce is the most abundant REE in the Earth's crust and typically exhibits the highest absolute concentration among the lanthanides. The Ce/La ratio and the cerium anomaly (Ce/Ce*) calculated from normalised values are sensitive to oxidation-reduction conditions during sediment formation and are documented in Sheet 04.
Pr_ppm	Decimal	Praseodymium concentration in ppm. Pr is a monoisotopic element (¹⁴¹Pr) measured without isobaric interference, making it analytically robust. Pr values are typically intermediate between La and Nd concentrations, and the Pr/Nd ratio provides a check on analytical accuracy and data consistency within the LREE sub-group.
Nd_ppm	Decimal	Neodymium concentration in ppm. Nd is geochemically significant as the radiogenic daughter of ¹⁴⁷Sm (through β-decay), and the Sm/Nd system provides independent isotopic provenance information. In this dataset Nd ranges from ~23 ppm (Group-D) to ~62 ppm (Group-E) and is one of the primary discriminants in PCA and LDA.
Sm_ppm	Decimal	Samarium concentration in ppm. Sm is one of the most geochemically important middle REEs and is the parent isotope in the Sm-Nd radiometric system. The La/Sm ratio (documented in Sheet 04 as LaN/SmN) provides a sensitive measure of LREE fractionation that discriminates between igneous-derived and pelitic source clays.
Eu_ppm	Decimal	Europium concentration in ppm. Eu is unique among the REEs in that it can occur as Eu²⁺ under reducing conditions, causing it to substitute into plagioclase feldspar and become decoupled from the other trivalent REEs. A negative europium anomaly (Eu/Eu*<1) indicates either a plagioclase-depleted source (e.g., granite) or reducing conditions during sediment formation, and is a key provenance discriminant.
Gd_ppm	Decimal	Gadolinium concentration in ppm. Gd serves as the pivot element between LREE and HREE sub-groups and is used in the calculation of both the Eu anomaly and the Gd anomaly (Gd/Gd*). Its concentration is sensitive to garnet fractionation in source rocks, with Gd depletion indicating derivation from garnet-bearing metamorphic or igneous sources.
Tb_ppm	Decimal	Terbium concentration in ppm, present at trace levels (typically 0.6–1.6 ppm in this dataset). Tb is routinely measured by ICP-MS without isobaric interferences and contributes to HREE sub-group totals and normalised pattern characterisation. It is utilised in the calculation of the Gd anomaly (Gd/Gd*) documented in Sheet 19.
Dy_ppm	Decimal	Dysprosium concentration in ppm. Dy is one of the more abundant HREE elements and contributes substantially to the ΣHREE total. The Tb/Yb ratio, which includes Dy as an intermediate point in the HREE pattern, is a useful discriminant of HREE fractionation patterns between provenance groups.
Ho_ppm	Decimal	Holmium concentration in ppm. Ho is strictly coupled geochemically with Y in natural systems (Y/Ho ratios of 26–30 are characteristic of terrestrial materials), and anomalous Y/Ho ratios documented in Sheet 19 may indicate post-depositional alteration or unusual source rock characteristics such as marine carbonate contributions.
Er_ppm	Decimal	Erbium concentration in ppm, a HREE element typically measured at concentrations of 2.0–4.7 ppm across the five provenance groups. Er participates in characterisation of the HREE slope from Dy to Lu, which reflects garnet stability in the source rock and sediment sorting processes.
Tm_ppm	Decimal	Thulium concentration in ppm, present at very low concentrations (0.28–0.66 ppm). Tm is the most analytically challenging lanthanide due to its low abundance, and its measurement requires high-sensitivity ICP-MS conditions with careful blank correction. Despite its low concentration, Tm is included to provide complete HREE pattern documentation.
Yb_ppm	Decimal	Ytterbium concentration in ppm, serving as the anchor of the HREE sub-group in normalised REE patterns. The LaN/YbN ratio (documented in Sheet 04) provides the fundamental measure of LREE/HREE fractionation and is one of the most discriminating parameters between provenance groups in multivariate statistical analyses.
Lu_ppm	Decimal	Lutetium concentration in ppm, the heaviest and least abundant stable lanthanide. Lu is geochemically paired with Hf (through the ¹⁷⁶Lu→¹⁷⁶Hf decay system) and its Lu/Hf ratio (documented in Sheet 19) may provide additional provenance information when combined with Hf measurements. Values range from 0.27–0.66 ppm across the five provenance groups.
ΣREE_ppm	Decimal	The sum of all fourteen measured REE concentrations (La through Lu) in ppm. ΣREE provides an overall measure of REE enrichment in the ceramic fabric and is highly group-discriminating, ranging from ~145 ppm (Group-D) to ~380 ppm (Group-E). Extremely high or low ΣREE values relative to the group centroid may indicate unusual source material or analytical issues.
ΣLREE	Decimal	The sum of light REE concentrations, defined here as La+Ce+Pr+Nd+Sm+Eu, expressed in ppm. ΣLREE constitutes the dominant fraction of total REE budget in most geological materials and is strongly controlled by accessory mineral phases such as monazite, allanite, and apatite in the source clay.
ΣHREE	Decimal	The sum of heavy REE concentrations, defined here as Gd+Tb+Dy+Ho+Er+Tm+Yb+Lu, expressed in ppm. ΣHREE is more sensitive to garnet and zircon in the source rock than ΣLREE, and systematic differences in ΣHREE between provenance groups reflect variations in accessory mineral assemblages in the parent geological formations.
LREE_HREE_ratio	Decimal	The ratio of ΣLREE to ΣHREE, providing a dimensionless measure of the degree of LREE enrichment relative to HREE. This ratio typically ranges from 8 to 18 in Anatolian ceramic fabrics and shows clear group separation: calcareous Tauride clays (Group-D) show relatively lower LREE/HREE ratios than illite-dominated Central Anatolian clays (Group-A).
Provenance_Group	Text	The geochemical provenance group assignment derived from multivariate statistical analysis of the complete REE dataset. Group assignments are cross-validated against those from major element (Sheet 02), strontium isotope (Sheet 05), and petrographic (Sheet 06) analyses, with concordance statistics documented in the Multi-Proxy Framework (Sheet 20).

 
Sheet 04: Chondrite_Normalised_REE

Chondrite normalisation of REE concentrations is the standard procedure for revealing the relative fractionation pattern of the lanthanides, independent of the Oddo-Harkins effect (which causes even-atomic-number elements to be more abundant than their odd-numbered neighbours). Normalisation is performed by dividing each measured ceramic REE concentration by the corresponding value in the CI chondrite reference material as published by Boynton (1984), which represents the primitive solar system REE composition. The resulting normalised values (denoted with the subscript N) reveal the characteristic REE pattern of the source clay, and anomalies in Ce and Eu provide geochemically diagnostic information about redox conditions and feldspar fractionation respectively. This sheet presents the complete suite of chondrite-normalised values together with calculated anomaly parameters and fractionation ratios.

Column Definitions
Column Name	Data Type	Description & Scientific Significance
Sample_ID	Text	Unique specimen identifier linking this sheet to the master Sample_Registry (Sheet 01) and to the raw REE concentration data in Sheet 03, from which all normalised values in this sheet are calculated.
LaN	Decimal	Lanthanum concentration normalised to the CI chondrite value of 0.367 ppm (Boynton 1984). LaN values in this dataset range from approximately 55 (Group-D) to 177 (Group-E), reflecting the degree of LREE enrichment of each ceramic's source clay relative to the primitive solar system composition.
CeN	Decimal	Cerium concentration normalised to the CI chondrite value of 0.957 ppm. CeN is used in the calculation of the Ce anomaly (CeN/CeN*), where CeN* is the interpolated value based on adjacent LaN and PrN. Significant Ce anomalies indicate either oxidising marine porewater conditions (positive anomaly) or reducing sedimentary conditions (negative anomaly) in the clay source environment.
PrN	Decimal	Praseodymium concentration normalised to 0.137 ppm. PrN serves as the interpolation anchor on the La side of the Ce anomaly calculation and provides a check on the smoothness of the LREE pattern. Pr is analytically robust as a monoisotopic element without isobaric interferences at mass 141.
NdN	Decimal	Neodymium concentration normalised to 0.711 ppm. NdN values contribute to the characterisation of the LREE slope from La to Sm in the chondrite-normalised pattern diagram. The NdN/SmN ratio is an additional fractionation indicator used in bivariate discrimination plots.
SmN	Decimal	Samarium concentration normalised to 0.231 ppm. SmN participates in the calculation of the Eu anomaly (EuN/EuN*, where EuN* = √(SmN × GdN)) and the LaN/SmN ratio. Sm normalised values typically show less inter-group variability than La and Ce, making them useful as a stability reference within the LREE sub-group.
EuN	Decimal	Europium concentration normalised to 0.0866 ppm. EuN is the numerator in the Eu anomaly calculation and shows the most diagnostic inter-group variation in chondrite-normalised space. Group-D (Tauride) ceramics exhibit the least negative Eu anomaly (EuN/EuN* closest to 1.0), while Group-E (Eastern Anatolian) samples show the most pronounced negative anomaly.
GdN	Decimal	Gadolinium concentration normalised to 0.306 ppm. GdN serves as the LREE/HREE boundary element in normalised REE patterns and is the geometric mean denominator partner with SmN in the Eu anomaly calculation. Gd occupies the inflection point of the chondrite-normalised REE pattern, making it critical for pattern shape characterisation.
TbN	Decimal	Terbium concentration normalised to 0.0589 ppm. TbN contributes to the HREE slope characterisation and is used in the calculation of the Gd anomaly (GdN/GdN* = GdN/√(SmN × TbN)), which is documented in Sheet 19. The Gd anomaly provides information on marine versus continental influences in the depositional history of the source clay.
DyN	Decimal	Dysprosium concentration normalised to 0.381 ppm. DyN continues the HREE pattern from Tb toward Ho and Er, and participates in the characterisation of the HREE slope that distinguishes garnet-buffered (steeply sloping HREE) from non-garnet (flat HREE) source rock compositions.
HoN	Decimal	Holmium concentration normalised to 0.0851 ppm. HoN, together with YN where measured, provides the Y/Ho discriminant between terrestrial and marine carbonate sources: terrestrial materials have Y/Ho ≈ 26–30, while marine carbonates show Y/Ho>35. This distinction is relevant to identifying calcareous clay sources of marine origin within the Tauride group.
ErN	Decimal	Erbium concentration normalised to 0.249 ppm. ErN contributes to HREE slope characterisation and provides the second-to-last anchor point before the heavier lanthanides Tm, Yb, and Lu. The Er/Yb ratio monitors the gradient of the HREE end-segment of the normalised pattern.
TmN	Decimal	Thulium concentration normalised to 0.0356 ppm. TmN values, though low in absolute terms, are included in the normalised dataset to provide a complete fourteen-element REE pattern for each specimen. Complete patterns are essential for accurate visual and statistical comparison with published reference datasets for Anatolian geological formations.
YbN	Decimal	Ytterbium concentration normalised to 0.248 ppm. YbN is the denominator of the LaN/YbN ratio, which is one of the most sensitive and widely applied measures of overall LREE/HREE fractionation. Group separation in LaN/YbN space is clear and statistically robust, making this ratio a primary discriminant in multi-element provenance plots.
LuN	Decimal	Lutetium concentration normalised to 0.0381 ppm. LuN terminates the chondrite-normalised REE pattern and, together with YbN, defines the final gradient of the HREE segment. The LuN value also participates in the Lu/Hf geochemical ratio used in crustal evolution studies, relevant to constraining the age and tectonic setting of the source geological unit.
CeN_CeN*	Decimal	The cerium anomaly, calculated as CeN/(0.5×LaN + 0.5×PrN), representing the deviation of the measured Ce concentration from the value predicted by linear interpolation between La and Pr in chondrite-normalised space. Values <0.90 indicate a negative Ce anomaly (reducing or continental source conditions); values >1.10 indicate a positive anomaly (oxidising marine conditions). Most Anatolian ceramic samples exhibit values close to 1.0 indicating negligible Ce anomalies.
EuN_EuN*	Decimal	The europium anomaly, calculated as EuN/√(SmN × GdN), representing the deviation of Eu from the predicted value interpolated from adjacent Sm and Gd in normalised space. Values <0.85 denote a significantly negative Eu anomaly, indicating source rock depleted in Eu through plagioclase fractionation (granites, rhyolites). This parameter is among the most powerful discriminants between granitic (negative Eu*) and more mafic or pelitic (less negative Eu*) clay source formations.
LaN_YbN	Decimal	The ratio of normalised La to normalised Yb concentrations (LaN/YbN), providing the overall measure of LREE enrichment relative to HREE in the chondrite-normalised pattern. Higher LaN/YbN values indicate greater LREE/HREE fractionation, characteristic of continental crustal sources. Values in this dataset range from ~8 (Tauride group) to ~22 (Eastern Anatolian group), with clear group separation confirming the utility of this parameter for provenance discrimination.
LaN_SmN	Decimal	The ratio of normalised La to normalised Sm concentrations, measuring the gradient of the LREE segment of the chondrite-normalised pattern. LaN/SmN values >4 indicate strong LREE fractionation typical of evolved crustal lithologies, while values <3 indicate flatter LREE patterns more characteristic of less differentiated source rocks. Group-C (Pontide) ceramics exhibit the steepest LREE slopes, consistent with their derivation from more evolved arc-related volcanic sources.
Provenance_Group	Text	The geochemical provenance group assignment for the specimen, as determined by multivariate statistical analysis. Inclusion of this field in the chondrite-normalised sheet facilitates direct comparison of normalised pattern parameters with group assignments derived from the full multi-proxy analysis documented in Sheet 20.

 
Sheet 05: Sr_Isotope_Data

Strontium isotope ratios (⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr) constitute an independent and complementary provenance proxy to REE fingerprinting. The ⁸⁷Sr isotope is the radiogenic daughter product of ⁸⁷Rb (rubidium-87) through β-decay, and the ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr ratio of a geological material reflects the time-integrated Rb/Sr ratio of its source: ancient, Rb-rich continental crustal rocks (granites, gneisses) yield high ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr ratios (>0.710), while younger, Rb-poor mantle-derived rocks (basalts, ophiolites) exhibit lower ratios (~0.703–0.706). In Anatolia, the major geological zones — Central Anatolian Crystalline Complex, Pontide Zone, Tauride Platform, Menderes Massif — possess characteristically distinct ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr signatures that are transmitted to clays derived through their weathering and are preserved in ceramic fabrics. Measurements were performed by Thermal Ionisation Mass Spectrometry (TIMS) or Multi-Collector ICP-MS (MC-ICP-MS), normalised to NIST SRM 987 (⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr = 0.710248), with internal precision reported as 2σ.

Sheet 06: Petrographic_Modal_Analysis

Petrographic thin-section analysis provides the physical and mineralogical characterisation of ceramic fabric that complements the geochemical and isotopic data presented in other sheets. Standard 30-µm polished thin sections were prepared from each specimen and examined under plane-polarised and cross-polarised light using a polarising petrographic microscope. Modal analysis was performed by point-counting a minimum of 200–400 points per section according to the modified Gazzi-Dickinson method, recording the proportions of all mineralogical and textural components including the fine-grained clay matrix. Grain size, sorting, and roundness characteristics were determined by visual comparison with standard reference charts. Estimated firing temperatures are derived from phase assemblage evidence and are cross-referenced with the dedicated firing temperature analysis in Sheet 08.

Column Definitions
Column Name	Data Type	Description & Scientific Significance
Sample_ID	Text	Unique specimen identifier linking each petrographic analysis to the master Sample_Registry (Sheet 01) and to complementary geochemical datasets.
Quartz_%	Decimal	The modal percentage of quartz grains (monocrystalline and polycrystalline) in the ceramic fabric as determined by point-counting. Quartz content ranges from approximately 15% to 45% across the dataset and is among the most variable fabric components. High quartz contents may reflect deliberate addition of sand temper or derivation from coarse-grained sandy source deposits.
K-Feldspar_%	Decimal	The modal percentage of potassium feldspar grains (orthoclase, microcline, sanidine) in the ceramic fabric. K-feldspar, together with plagioclase, provides textural evidence for the igneous or metamorphic character of mineral inclusions. K-feldspar contents above ~15% suggest granite-derived or arkosic source materials.
Plagioclase_%	Decimal	The modal percentage of plagioclase feldspar grains in the ceramic fabric. Plagioclase occurrence indicates derivation from igneous or intermediate metamorphic source rocks and correlates inversely with CIA values (Sheet 02): high plagioclase contents correspond to lower CIA values, reflecting less weathered source materials.
Calcite_%	Decimal	The modal percentage of calcite in the ceramic fabric, including both detrital carbonate grains and sparite/micrite fragments. Calcite content is distinctly elevated in Tauride group (Group-D) ceramics due to their derivation from calcite-rich source clays of the Tauride carbonate platform. Calcite is largely decomposed to CaO above ~850°C, and its presence in fired ceramics constrains maximum firing temperature.
Dolomite_%	Decimal	The modal percentage of dolomite in the ceramic fabric. Dolomite persists to higher firing temperatures than calcite and its presence alongside calcite decomposition products provides a refined thermal indicator. Dolomite is most abundant in Tauride group ceramics, consistent with the dolomitic limestone formations of the Tauride Platform.
Mica_%	Decimal	The combined modal percentage of muscovite, biotite, and other phyllosilicate mineral flakes. Mica content reflects derivation from metamorphic or granitic source rocks and is elevated in Central Anatolian (Group-A) and Pontide (Group-C) ceramics, consistent with their mica schist and granitic source formations. Mica dehydration begins above ~700°C, and recrystallised mica may indicate firing temperatures in the 850–950°C range.
Amphibole_%	Decimal	The modal percentage of amphibole (hornblende, actinolite, tremolite) in the ceramic fabric. Amphibole presence indicates derivation from intermediate igneous, amphibolite-facies metamorphic, or skarn-related source materials. Amphibole is thermally unstable above ~900°C and its survival places an upper bound on the firing temperature.
Pyroxene_%	Decimal	The modal percentage of pyroxene minerals (augite, diopside, enstatite) in the ceramic fabric. Pyroxene occurrence indicates derivation from mafic igneous sources or from re-fired ceramics where high-temperature pyroxene phases have crystallised from the melt. De novo pyroxene crystallisation above ~1000°C is a diagnostic high-firing indicator.
Clay_Matrix_%	Decimal	The modal percentage of fine-grained clay matrix material — the amorphous to microcrystalline groundmass of the ceramic body — as distinguished from coarser mineral inclusions. Clay matrix content typically ranges from 35% to 65% and reflects the proportion of the original clay mineral component. Matrix properties including birefringence and optical isotropy encode firing temperature information.
Opaques_%	Decimal	The modal percentage of opaque mineral phases, primarily iron oxides (magnetite, haematite, ilmenite) and sulphides. Opaque content, typically 1–5%, provides a complementary constraint on firing conditions: magnetite occurrence indicates reducing firing, haematite dominance indicates oxidising conditions, consistent with the firing atmosphere assessment in the Munsell colour and firing temperature analysis.
Others_%	Decimal	The modal percentage of all remaining mineral phases not individually categorised, including tourmaline, zircon, apatite, garnet, epidote, and unidentified grains. The 'Others' category represents heavy and accessory mineral phases that, while present in trace amounts, may carry important provenance information (e.g., garnet indicating derivation from garnet-mica schist).

This field serves as a quality-control check on the completeness and consistency of the modal analysis.
Points_Counted	Integer	The total number of points counted during the modal analysis, ranging from 200 to 400 per thin section. Section_Thick_mm	Decimal	The thickness of the polished thin section in millimetres, typically standardised at 0.025–0.035 mm (25–35 µm) for transmitted light petrography. Grain_Size_Mean_mm	Decimal	The mean grain size of non-clay mineral inclusions in the ceramic fabric, determined by ocular micrometer measurement of representative grains in thin section. Grain size distribution is central to fabric classification and temper characterisation.

Categories range from Angular through Sub-angular, Sub-rounded, Rounded, to Well-rounded. Angular grains suggest minimal sedimentary transport and may indicate local clay sources or crushed rock temper; well-rounded grains indicate prolonged fluvial or aeolian transport.
Fabric_Type	Text	The overall fabric type classification based on the integration of compositional, textural, and technological observations. Fabric types include Calcareous Fine, Non-calcareous Medium, Coarse Gritty, Sandy Micaceous, Volcanic Tempered, Calcitic Tempered, and Iron-rich Red. Fabric type is the primary petrographic taxonomic unit and correlates with both clay source characteristics and ceramic production technology.
Est_Firing_Temp_C	Integer	The estimated firing temperature in degrees Celsius, derived from phase assemblage evidence observable in thin section (presence/absence of calcite, amphibole, pyroxene; degree of matrix vitrification; mullite formation) and cross-referenced with TGA/DTA data in Sheet 08. Values range from 750°C to 1050°C across the dataset, reflecting the broad range of firing practices represented in Bronze Age Anatolian ceramic assemblages.

 
Sheet 07: Grain_Size_Distribution

Grain size analysis of the ceramic fabric and associated clay source sediments, performed by laser diffraction particle size analysis and complementary dry-sieve techniques, provides quantitative characterisation of the textural properties of the paste. Grain size distribution parameters — including percentile values (D10, D50, D90), phi-scale statistics (mean, standard deviation, skewness, kurtosis), and fractional proportions (clay, silt, sand fractions) — reflect the sedimentological history of the clay source, including transport energy, depositional environment, and post-depositional modification. Systematic differences in grain size distributions between provenance groups provide additional textural discriminants to complement geochemical and isotopic signatures.

Sample_ID	Text	Unique specimen identifier linking each grain size analysis to the master Sample_Registry (Sheet 01).
D10_µm	Decimal	The 10th percentile grain size in micrometres: 10% of the sample volume consists of particles smaller than this value. D10 characterises the fine end of the grain size distribution and is sensitive to the clay and fine silt fraction content. Lower D10 values indicate finer-grained fabric compositions typical of lacustrine or low-energy fluvial clay sources.
D25_µm	Decimal	The 25th percentile grain size in micrometres, also known as Q1 (first quartile). D25 delineates the boundary between the fine fraction and the lower quartile of the distribution and contributes to the calculation of the interquartile range used in sorting assessment.

D50 is the primary grain size descriptor and is used in the calculation of the phi-scale mean (MΦ = −log₂(D50/1000)). D50 values in the dataset range from approximately 25 µm (fine silt) to over 100 µm (coarse silt to fine sand).
D75_µm	Decimal	The 75th percentile grain size in micrometres (Q3). Together with D25, D75 defines the interquartile range (IQR = D75 − D25) which provides a robust, outlier-insensitive measure of central sorting. D75 characterises the coarser modal fraction of the inclusion population.

Leptokurtic distributions indicate strong unimodal sorting, while platykurtic distributions suggest bimodal or polymodal grain populations.
Clay_frac_%	Decimal	The percentage of the sample volume in the clay particle size fraction (<2 µm). Silt_frac_%	Decimal	The percentage of the sample volume in the silt fraction (2–63 µm). Silt is typically the dominant fraction in ceramic pastes and includes quartz, feldspar, and clay mineral particles. The ratio of clay to silt fraction is a primary indicator of the depositional energy and processing history of the clay source.
Fine_Sand_%	Decimal	The percentage of the sample volume in the fine sand fraction (63–250 µm). Fine sand in ceramic fabrics may reflect natural coarse inclusions in the clay source or deliberate sand temper addition. The boundary between natural inclusions and added temper is often indeterminate without experimental reference data.
Medium_Sand_%	Decimal	The percentage of the sample volume in the medium sand fraction (250–500 µm). Significant medium sand fractions (>10%) typically indicate deliberate coarse temper addition, as most natural clay source deposits contain limited medium sand. The presence of angular medium sand grains of consistent mineralogy is strong evidence for intentional sand temper.
Coarse_Sand_%	Decimal	The percentage of the sample volume in the coarse sand fraction (500–2000 µm). Coarse sand inclusions visible to the naked eye are frequently interpreted as deliberate chaff, mineral grog, or rock-fragment temper. Their mineralogical character, assessed petrographically in Sheet 06, provides information on temper source and possibly on exchange of ceramic production technology.
Gravel_%	Decimal	The percentage of the sample volume coarser than 2 mm, typically near zero in ceramic fabrics. Sorting_Class	Text	The qualitative sorting class corresponding to the calculated SD_phi value, assigned according to the Folk and Ward (1957) verbal class scale: Very well sorted, Well sorted, Moderately sorted, Poorly sorted, or Very poorly sorted. Folk_Classification	Text	The textural classification of the bulk sample following Folk's (1954) scheme for sedimentary rocks, applied here to the ceramic paste composition. Categories include Sandy silt, Silty sand, Fine sandy loam, Sandy clay loam, Clay loam, Silty clay, Sandy clay, and Loam. Folk classification provides a standardised textural descriptor for the ceramic paste composition.
Provenance_Group	Text	The assigned provenance group for cross-referencing grain size characteristics with geochemical and isotopic provenance signatures. Systematic differences in grain size parameters between groups reflect the contrasting sedimentological character of clay source deposits in different geological zones of Anatolia.

 
Sheet 08: Firing_Temperature_Analysis

The determination of ceramic firing temperatures and conditions provides essential technological information that is methodologically distinct from but complementary to provenance analysis. Firing temperature affects the stability of geochemical proxies (particularly mobile elements) and constrains the technological capabilities of Bronze Age potters. This sheet presents firing temperature estimates derived from thermogravimetric analysis (TGA), differential thermal analysis (DTA), controlled refiring experiments, X-ray powder diffraction phase analysis, and scanning electron microscopy of ceramic microstructures. Estimates are expressed as minimum–maximum ranges with a best estimate value, reflecting the inherent uncertainty in firing temperature determination from archaeological ceramics.

Column Definitions
Column Name	Data Type	Description & Scientific Significance
Sample_ID	Text	Unique specimen identifier.
Min_Temp_C	Integer	The minimum estimated firing temperature in degrees Celsius, representing the lower bound of the inferred firing temperature range. This value is typically constrained by the thermal decomposition behaviour of minerals that are absent in the specimen (e.g., if calcite is decomposed, the minimum temperature is ≥850°C).
Max_Temp_C	Integer	The maximum estimated firing temperature in degrees Celsius, representing the upper bound of the inferred range. This is constrained by the absence of high-temperature phases that would form at greater temperatures (e.g., if mullite is absent, the maximum is typically <1100°C in aluminous ceramics).
Best_Est_Temp_C	Integer	The best estimate of firing temperature in degrees Celsius, representing the modal or most probable value within the estimated range. This value integrates evidence from multiple methods and is the primary temperature parameter used in comparative analyses of firing technology across sites and periods.
Method	Text	The analytical method or combination of methods used to estimate the firing temperature. Methods include TGA/DTA (thermogravimetric and differential thermal analysis), refiring experiments, Mossbauer spectroscopy (for iron phase characterisation), XRPD phase analysis (X-ray powder diffraction), SEM-EDS microstructure analysis, and combined approaches. Method selection affects the reliability and precision of the temperature estimate.
Firing_Atm	Text	The inferred firing atmosphere during the principal thermal treatment, classified as Oxidising, Reducing, Alternating Oxidising/Reducing, Neutral, or Incomplete Oxidation. Atmosphere determination is based on Munsell core colour, iron oxide phase identification (haematite vs. magnetite), and refiring experiment results. The firing atmosphere affects trace element mobility and is relevant to the interpretation of REE concentrations in highly reduced ceramics.
Vitrification_Degree_%	Decimal	An estimate of the degree of vitrification (glass formation) in the ceramic body, expressed as a percentage of the cross-sectional area occupied by vitrified glass phase as observed in SEM backscatter imaging or high-magnification optical microscopy. Higher vitrification degrees indicate higher firing temperatures or longer firing durations.
Apparent_Porosity_%	Decimal	The apparent porosity of the ceramic body, measured by the Archimedes water absorption method (ASTM C373). Apparent porosity decreases with increasing firing temperature as clay mineral dehydroxylation and sintering reduce interconnected void space. Values range from ~5% (high-fired, well-vitrified sherds) to ~40% (low-fired, poorly sintered wares) in this dataset.
Water_Absorption_%	Decimal	The percentage increase in mass of the ceramic specimen after immersion in boiling water for two hours, providing a practical measure of open porosity accessible to water. Water absorption is inversely correlated with firing temperature and degree of sintering, and provides a proxy for ceramic durability and functionality.
Core_Color	Text	The Munsell-class colour of the ceramic fabric in the core zone of the cross-section, as observed under standardised lighting. Core colour reflects the firing atmosphere and temperature in the innermost part of the vessel wall: grey or black cores indicate incomplete organic burnout or reducing conditions, while red-orange cores indicate oxidising conditions at sufficient temperature.
Surface_Color	Text	The Munsell-class colour of the outer surface or applied slip, reflecting the final firing atmosphere and any surface treatment applied before or during firing. Discordance between core and surface colour (sandwich fabrics) indicates changes in firing atmosphere during the firing cycle, a common observation in Bronze Age Anatolian ceramics fired in open bonfires or simple kilns.
Quartz_Inversion_Ev	Text	The presence, absence, or partial expression of the alpha-beta quartz inversion at 573°C as detected by DTA or refiring experiments. The quartz inversion is Present in ceramics fired below 573°C, Absent above this temperature (quartz reversion is complete on cooling), or Partial where rapid cooling has frozen intermediate inversion states. This binary indicator provides a fundamental temperature boundary constraint.
Carbonate_Decomp_Ev	Text	Evidence for the decomposition of carbonate minerals (calcite decomposing at ~800–900°C, dolomite at ~700–800°C), assessed as Complete, Partial, or Absent based on DTA endotherm magnitude and XRD phase analysis. Fe_Oxide_Phase	Text	The dominant iron oxide phase identified by Mossbauer spectroscopy or XRD, including Haematite dominant (oxidising conditions, T>600°C), Magnetite traces (reducing conditions), Ilmenite (Ti-Fe phase, neutral to reducing), Goethite partial (low-temperature, incomplete dehydration, T<300°C), Haematite+Magnetite (alternating atmosphere), Wustite traces (strongly reducing, high temperature), Maghemite, and Undifferentiated. Iron oxide phase provides a sensitive indicator of firing atmosphere and temperature.
Mullite_Formation	Text	Assessment of mullite (Al₆Si₂O₁₃) formation as Absent, Incipient, or Present based on XRD analysis. Mullite typically begins to form above ~1000°C in aluminosilicate ceramics and its presence indicates high-temperature firing conditions. The presence of even trace mullite significantly constrains the minimum firing temperature in the upper range assessed in this dataset.
Kiln_Type_Inferred	Text	The type of firing installation inferred from the firing temperature range, temperature uniformity (core-to-surface gradient), and firing atmosphere data. Categories include Single-chamber updraught kiln, Two-chamber kiln, Open bonfire, Pit firing, Kiln with perforated floor, Enclosed clamp, and Unknown. The inferred kiln type has implications for the scale and specialisation of ceramic production.
Duration_Est_hrs	Decimal	The estimated firing duration in hours, derived from microstructural evidence of sintering extent and grain boundary diffusion rates calibrated to laboratory experiments. Typical Bronze Age kiln firings are estimated at 2–12 hours for the main firing phase, with extended smouldering periods of up to 48 hours in some bonfire and pit-firing scenarios.
Firing_Quality	Text	An overall qualitative assessment of firing consistency and quality, classified as High (uniform temperature throughout the vessel wall, consistent colour, fully sintered), Moderate (minor colour variations, adequate sintering), Low (significant colour variations, poor sintering, evidence of underfiring), or Variable (heterogeneous firing quality within a single specimen, suggesting unstable kiln conditions).
Refired_Temp_C	Integer	The temperature in degrees Celsius at which the specimen was refired in controlled laboratory refiring experiments, used to assess changes in colour, microstructure, and mineralogy as a function of temperature. Comparison of refired and original specimens enables identification of the original firing temperature as the point at which no further changes are induced.
Provenance_Group	Text	The provenance group assignment, included to enable assessment of whether significant differences in firing technology exist between provenance groups, which might imply different pottery production traditions or different workshop practices associated with specific clay sources or cultural groups.

 
Sheet 09: Geochemical_Indices

Geochemical provenance indices derived from trace element ratios provide powerful discriminants for distinguishing between ceramic fabrics derived from different tectonic source terranes and geological settings. Ratios such as La/Sc, Th/Sc, La/Co, and Th/Co exploit contrasts in element behaviour between continental crust-derived and mantle-derived geological materials, while Cr/Ni and V/Cr ratios discriminate between mafic and evolved compositions. These ratios are substantially immobile during sedimentary processes and ceramic firing, making them robust provenance indicators. The CIA and ICV indices quantify chemical weathering and source maturity respectively, complementing the textural information in Sheet 07.

Column Definitions
Column Name	Data Type	Description & Scientific Significance
Sample_ID	Text	Unique specimen identifier.
CIA	Decimal	Chemical Index of Alteration: Al₂O₃/(Al₂O₃+CaO+Na₂O+K₂O)×100 in molecular proportions. CIA measures the degree of chemical weathering and increases from ~50 (fresh igneous rocks) toward 100 (residual kaolinite). Values >75 indicate mature, intensely weathered clay sources; values <60 indicate relatively unweathered, first-cycle sediment sources.
ICV	Decimal	Index of Compositional Variability: (Fe₂O₃+K₂O+Na₂O+CaO+MgO+TiO₂)/Al₂O₃. ICV>1 indicates first-cycle or immature sediments; ICV<1 indicates mature, feldspar-poor, clay-rich compositions. ICV combined with CIA effectively plots source material evolution from igneous parent to fully mature pelite.
PIA	Decimal	Plagioclase Index of Alteration: (Al₂O₃−K₂O)/(Al₂O₃+CaO+Na₂O−K₂O)×100. PIA specifically monitors the alteration of plagioclase feldspar to secondary clay minerals, providing a complementary measure to CIA that is less sensitive to K-bearing phases. PIA is particularly useful for discriminating between plagioclase-rich (low PIA) and K-feldspar-dominant (CIA and PIA diverge) source lithologies.
WIP	Decimal	Weathering Index of Parker (1970): (2Na₂O/0.35 + MgO/0.9 + 2K₂O/0.25 + CaO/0.7)×100, calculated in molecular proportions. WIP decreases monotonically with increasing weathering intensity from ~100 (unweathered rock) toward 0 (completely leached residual clay). WIP is complementary to CIA and ICV and provides a third independent measure of source material weathering maturity.
Al2O3_TiO2	Decimal	The Al₂O₃/TiO₂ weight ratio, serving as a discriminant between different source rock types: values of 3–8 are typical of mafic to intermediate rocks; 8–21 of average upper continental crust; >21 of highly differentiated granitic sources. This ratio is immobile under most weathering and diagenetic conditions and provides a reliable geological fingerprint preserved in ceramic fabrics.
La_Sc	Decimal	The La/Sc ratio, one of the most powerful tectonic discriminants available from geochemical data. Sc is concentrated in mafic-ultramafic rocks (>40 ppm), while La is enriched in evolved continental crust. La/Sc values of <2 indicate derivation from mafic/ophiolitic sources; values >10 indicate felsic continental crustal sources. Group-E (Eastern Anatolian) ceramics exhibit the highest La/Sc ratios consistent with their ancient crystalline basement source.
Th_Sc	Decimal	The Th/Sc ratio, another primary tectonic setting discriminant. Th is highly enriched in continental crust and Sc in mantle-derived rocks. Th/Sc <0.3 indicates active continental margin or island arc sources; values 0.3–1.0 indicate passive margin or mixed sources; values >1.0 indicate upper continental crustal or recycled sediment sources. This ratio shows clear and consistent inter-group differences in this dataset.
Zr_Sc	Decimal	The Zr/Sc ratio, reflecting the degree of sedimentary recycling and heavy mineral concentration (Zr in zircon, Sc in mafic minerals). Zr/Sc increases through sedimentary cycles as Sc is diluted and Zr-bearing zircon is concentrated by hydrodynamic sorting. Values >40 indicate mature, multiply recycled sediments; values <15 indicate immature, first-cycle materials.
La_Co	Decimal	The La/Co ratio, discriminating between continental crustal sources (high La, low Co; La/Co >2) and mafic or ophiolitic sources (low La, high Co; La/Co <1). Co is concentrated in mantle-derived rocks and ultramafic cumulates, and its dilution in evolved crustal lithologies produces systematically elevated La/Co ratios in ceramics derived from granitic or metamorphic clay sources.
Th_Co	Decimal	The Th/Co ratio, combining the HFSE signature of Th (continental crust indicator) with the transition metal signature of Co (mafic source indicator). Th/Co >1.0 indicates dominantly continental crustal source material; values <0.3 indicate mafic contributions. This ratio effectively separates Group-E (Eastern Anatolian, high Th/Co) from Group-D (Tauride, low Th/Co) ceramics.
Th_Cr	Decimal	The Th/Cr ratio, where Cr is concentrated in ultramafic and mafic rocks (komatiites, peridotites, chromitites). Elevated Th/Cr ratios indicate derivation from evolved crustal sources with low Cr; low Th/Cr ratios indicate contributions from ophiolitic or mafic arc sources. The ratio is particularly discriminating for ceramics derived from ophiolite-proximal clay sources in Eastern Anatolia.
Cr_Ni_ratio	Decimal	The Cr/Ni ratio, both elements being transition metals concentrated in mafic and ultramafic rocks but with different behaviour during magmatic differentiation. Cr/Ni ratios close to 1 suggest an ultramafic source (dunite, harzburgite); ratios of 2–5 suggest mafic cumulates; higher ratios (>10) suggest differentiated or sedimentarily processed sources. This ratio complements the La/Sc and Th/Sc tectonic discriminants.
V_Cr_ratio	Decimal	The V/Cr ratio, providing a redox-sensitive provenance discriminant. V is enriched relative to Cr under oxidising conditions in sedimentary basins (V³⁺/V⁴⁺/V⁵⁺ mobility), while Cr remains relatively immobile. V/Cr >4.25 is used as an indicator of anoxic depositional conditions in geochemical literature; values <2 indicate oxic sedimentary environments for the clay source.
Th_ppm	Decimal	Thorium concentration in parts per million, a lithophile incompatible element concentrated in continental crust through magmatic differentiation. Th concentrations range from ~8 to ~20 ppm across the provenance groups and are a primary component in the Th/Sc, Th/Co, and Th/U ratios used as tectonic discriminants.
Sc_ppm	Decimal	Scandium concentration in parts per million, a transition metal element compatible in mafic minerals (clinopyroxene, orthopyroxene, amphibole). Sc concentrations inversely correlate with the degree of magmatic differentiation and sedimentary maturity, providing the mafic component denominator in La/Sc and Th/Sc ratios.
Zr_ppm	Decimal	Zirconium concentration in parts per million, largely controlled by zircon abundance in the ceramic fabric. Zr is highly immobile during weathering and diagenesis and its concentration reflects both source rock type (high in granites and rhyolites) and sedimentary sorting history (zircon concentration by hydrodynamic processes).
Cr_ppm	Decimal	Chromium concentration in parts per million, a transition metal element concentrated in ultramafic rocks (>1000 ppm in dunites) and to a lesser extent in mafic rocks (200–500 ppm in basalts). Systematically elevated Cr values in Eastern Anatolian ceramics reflect the proximity of their clay source areas to ophiolitic sequences in southeastern Anatolia.
Ni_ppm	Decimal	Nickel concentration in parts per million, the partner transition metal to Cr in ophiolite and mafic source discrimination. Ni/Cr ratios in this dataset correlate with the Cr/Ni ratios documented in the preceding column. Elevated Ni together with elevated Cr unambiguously indicates an ophiolitic or ultramafic source component in the ceramic clay.
Provenance_Group	Text	The assigned provenance group, enabling systematic comparison of geochemical index values across the five defined groups and assessment of the discriminatory power of individual indices within the multi-proxy framework.

 
Sheet 10: PCA_Results

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) was applied to the combined standardised dataset of REE concentrations, major element oxides, and selected trace element ratios in order to reduce dimensionality, reveal underlying structure in the geochemical variation, and identify the analytical variables most responsible for discrimination between provenance groups. The analysis was performed on a correlation matrix (to equalise variable scales) using centred log-ratio transformation of compositional data following Aitchison (1986). The first five principal components are presented here, along with their associated variance explained and the dominant loading variables. Mahalanobis distances from group centroids and ellipse membership statistics provide quantitative assessments of each specimen's assignment confidence.

Column Definitions
Column Name	Data Type	Description & Scientific Significance
Sample_ID	Text	Unique specimen identifier.
PC1_score	Decimal	The score of each specimen on the first principal component, accounting for the largest proportion of total variance in the geochemical dataset (32–42% depending on variable selection). PC1 primarily reflects the REE enrichment gradient from LREE-poor (Tauride group) to LREE-rich (Eastern Anatolian) specimens and correlates strongly with ΣREE, La, Ce, and Nd.
PC2_score	Decimal	The score on the second principal component, accounting for 18–26% of total variance. PC2 primarily captures the major element variation axis from calcareous/mafic (high CaO, MgO) to silicic/felsic (high SiO₂, K₂O) compositions, effectively separating the Tauride group from the Central Anatolian group in a plane perpendicular to PC1.
PC3_score	Decimal	The score on the third principal component, accounting for 10–16% of total variance. PC3 captures residual variance not explained by PC1 and PC2, often reflecting minor variations in REE anomaly values (Eu/Eu*, Ce/Ce*) and trace element ratios (La/Sc, Th/Sc) that discriminate between groups with otherwise similar bulk REE and major element compositions.
PC4_score	Decimal	The score on the fourth principal component, explaining approximately 7–12% of total variance. PC4 captures fine-scale compositional variation within groups and is less interpretable in terms of geological source differences, but contributes to total variance explained and is included for completeness in statistical comparison with published PCA solutions.
PC5_score	Decimal	The score on the fifth principal component, explaining approximately 4–8% of total variance. PC5 and higher components primarily represent noise and intra-source variability. Inclusion of PC5 brings cumulative variance explained to typically >85% of total geochemical dataset variance for well-resolved provenance groups.
PC1_var_%	Decimal	The percentage of total dataset variance explained by PC1. Values in this dataset range from 32% to 42%, reflecting the dominant role of the bulk REE concentration gradient in structuring the overall compositional variation. Higher PC1 variance percentages indicate better separation between the most compositionally distinct provenance groups.
PC2_var_%	Decimal	The percentage of total variance explained by PC2, ranging from 18% to 26%. PC2 variance reflects the importance of the major element calcareous/silicic gradient in the dataset. The ratio of PC1 to PC2 variance provides information on the relative importance of REE versus major element variation for provenance discrimination.
PC3_var_%	Decimal	The percentage of total variance explained by PC3, ranging from 10% to 16%. The cumulative variance of PC1+PC2+PC3 (recorded in the following column) determines how much of the total geochemical variation is captured in the primary three-dimensional PCA space used for provenance classification.
Cumul_var_PC3_%	Decimal	The cumulative percentage of total variance explained by PC1, PC2, and PC3 combined. Values of 60–80% are typical for well-resolved geochemical provenance datasets. Cumulative variance below 60% in the first three components would indicate either complex multi-group structures or significant intra-group variation that requires additional components for adequate representation.
PC1_dominant_load	Text	The analytical variable with the highest absolute loading value on PC1, indicating the variable most responsible for the variance captured by this component. Typical dominant PC1 loadings in REE-augmented ceramic datasets include LaN/YbN, La_ppm, ΣREE, or Ce_ppm, all reflecting the LREE enrichment gradient.
PC2_dominant_load	Text	The analytical variable with the highest absolute loading on PC2. In datasets combining REE with major elements, dominant PC2 loadings frequently include SiO₂, Al₂O₃, K₂O/Na₂O, or CaO/Al₂O₃ ratios reflecting the major element variation axis.
PC3_dominant_load	Text	The analytical variable with the highest absolute loading on PC3. PC3 loadings often include anomaly parameters (Eu/Eu*, Ce/Ce*) or tectonic discriminant ratios (La/Sc, Th/Sc) that are orthogonal to the primary REE enrichment and major element axes captured by PC1 and PC2.
Mahal_dist_centroid	Decimal	The Mahalanobis distance of the specimen from the centroid of its assigned provenance group in the full PCA score space, accounting for the covariance structure of each group. Mahalanobis distance is the preferred metric for classification in multivariate compositional space because it correctly accounts for differential variance along each PC axis.
In_95pct_ellipse	Text	A YES/NO flag indicating whether the specimen plots within the 95% confidence ellipse of its assigned provenance group in the PC1-PC2 biplot. Specimens plotting outside this ellipse are compositional outliers relative to their assigned group and may represent genuine outliers, misclassified specimens, or samples from mixed clay sources.
In_99pct_ellipse	Text	A YES/NO flag indicating whether the specimen plots within the 99% confidence ellipse of its assigned provenance group. Specimens plotting outside the 99% ellipse are extreme statistical outliers that should be prioritised for reanalysis and re-evaluation of their provenance assignment in the multi-proxy synthesis.
PCA_cluster	Text	The cluster label assigned to the specimen in PCA score space, corresponding to the geographically and geologically defined provenance group. PCA cluster labels are derived independently from hierarchical cluster analysis applied to PC1-PC5 scores, providing a data-driven validation of the geologically informed group definitions.
Nearest_group	Text	The provenance group whose centroid is closest to the specimen in Mahalanobis distance, which may differ from the assigned group for borderline specimens. Discordance between the assigned group and the nearest group provides a measure of classification uncertainty and flags specimens requiring attention in the multi-proxy integration (Sheet 20).
Confidence_%	Decimal	A percentage confidence value for the PCA-based provenance assignment, estimated from the posterior probability of the specimen belonging to its assigned group based on its Mahalanobis distance from all group centroids. Values above 90% indicate high-confidence assignments; values below 70% indicate ambiguous classifications that benefit most from multi-proxy integration.
Provenance_Group	Text	The final assigned provenance group for cross-referencing PCA results with other analytical methods in the multi-proxy framework of Sheet 20.

 
Sheet 11: Discriminant_Analysis

Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA) is applied to the complete geochemical dataset as a supervised classification method that maximises the ratio of between-group to within-group variance, thereby identifying linear combinations of variables (discriminant functions) that most effectively separate the a priori defined provenance groups. Unlike PCA (which maximises total variance without reference to group structure), LDA is specifically optimised for group discrimination and provides direct probability-based classification assessments. The analysis employs a five-group model with cross-validation by jackknife resampling and 10-fold cross-validation to estimate real classification accuracy. Wilks' lambda and associated F-statistics assess the statistical significance of the discriminant functions.

Column Definitions
Column Name	Data Type	Description & Scientific Significance
Sample_ID	Text	Unique specimen identifier.
LD1_score	Decimal	The score of each specimen on the first linear discriminant function (LD1), which by definition captures the largest proportion of between-group variance. LD1 primarily reflects the most powerful single discriminant direction in the multi-dimensional geochemical space, analogous in geometric terms to the axis along which the five group centroids are most widely separated.
LD2_score	Decimal	The score on the second linear discriminant function (LD2), orthogonal to LD1. LD2 captures the next largest proportion of between-group variance and is needed to separate groups that overlap along LD1. Typically LD1 and LD2 together capture >90% of discriminating between-group variance in five-group ceramic provenance LDAs.
LD3_score	Decimal	The score on the third linear discriminant function, one of four possible discriminant axes in a five-group analysis. LD3 captures residual between-group variance not captured by LD1 and LD2, and is most relevant for separating groups that are closely similar in LD1–LD2 space.
Prior_Group	Text	The a priori provenance group assignment of the specimen used as input to the LDA model. In this dataset, Prior_Group is identical to the final Provenance_Group assignment determined by the overall multi-proxy analysis, reflecting the supervised nature of the classification procedure.
Posterior_Group	Text	The provenance group to which the specimen is assigned by the LDA classifier based on its posterior probability, which is the group with the highest computed posterior probability given the specimen's discriminant function scores. Discordance between Prior_Group and Posterior_Group identifies misclassified specimens.
Prob_GrpA_%	Decimal	The posterior probability (0–100%) that the specimen belongs to Group-A (Central-CACC), computed from the Mahalanobis distances to all five group centroids in discriminant function space using Bayes' theorem with equal prior probabilities. The five group probability values (Prob_GrpA through Prob_GrpE) sum to 100%.
Prob_GrpB_%	Decimal	The posterior probability that the specimen belongs to Group-B (Western-Aegean). High values indicate specimens with compositions closely resembling the Aegean-zone ceramic reference group.
Prob_GrpC_%	Decimal	The posterior probability that the specimen belongs to Group-C (Pontide).
Prob_GrpD_%	Decimal	The posterior probability that the specimen belongs to Group-D (Tauride). Specimens with high Prob_GrpD typically display elevated CaO, MgO, and low REE concentrations consistent with the carbonate-platform geological setting of the Tauride clay sources.
Prob_GrpE_%	Decimal	The posterior probability that the specimen belongs to Group-E (Eastern-Anatolia). High Prob_GrpE values are associated with specimens showing elevated ΣREE, high Th/Sc, and elevated ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr ratios characteristic of the ancient crystalline basement formations of eastern Anatolia.
Correct_Class	Text	A YES/NO flag indicating whether the LDA posterior classification (Posterior_Group) matches the a priori group assignment (Prior_Group). The overall correct classification rate across all specimens provides the LDA model's apparent classification accuracy, which should be validated by jackknife and cross-validation statistics to avoid overfitting.
Mahal_dist_LD	Decimal	The Mahalanobis distance of the specimen from the centroid of its assigned group in the discriminant function space (LD1, LD2, LD3). This is distinct from the PCA-based Mahalanobis distance in Sheet 10 and is computed in the discriminant function coordinate system, which is optimised for group separation rather than total variance representation.
Jackknife_Class	Text	The group assignment of the specimen under leave-one-out (jackknife) cross-validation, where the LDA model is refitted on all specimens except the one being classified. Jackknife classification provides an unbiased estimate of classification accuracy that accounts for the inflated accuracy of apparent (re-substitution) classification.
CV_10fold_Class	Text	The group assignment of the specimen under 10-fold cross-validation, where the dataset is repeatedly partitioned into 10 equal subsets, the model trained on 9 subsets, and the remaining subset used for independent classification. 10-fold cross-validation provides a practical estimate of classification accuracy for datasets of the size encountered in Bronze Age Anatolian ceramic provenance studies.
Wilks_Lambda	Decimal	Wilks' lambda statistic for the discriminant function, measuring the proportion of total variance in the discriminant scores that is attributable to within-group variance. Lambda ranges from 0 (perfect discrimination) to 1 (no discrimination). Values below 0.3 indicate very strong between-group separation; values above 0.7 indicate relatively weak discrimination.
F_statistic	Decimal	The F-statistic associated with the Wilks' lambda test of significance, testing the null hypothesis that all group centroids are equal in discriminant function space. High F-values (>10) with low p-values confirm statistically significant group separation and validate the geological coherence of the defined provenance groups.
p_value	Decimal	The p-value associated with the F-statistic, representing the probability of observing the degree of group separation by chance under the null hypothesis of equal group centroids. Values below 0.001 indicate statistically highly significant group separation, as expected for geochemically well-defined provenance groups with distinct geological origins.
Eigenvalue	Decimal	The eigenvalue of the discriminant function, measuring the ratio of between-group to within-group variance captured by each function. Higher eigenvalues indicate greater discriminating power. Eigenvalue magnitudes are proportional to the discriminant function's contribution to overall group separation and are used to assess the relative importance of LD1, LD2, and LD3.
Provenance_Group	Text	The final multi-proxy provenance group assignment for cross-referencing with the LDA classification results.

 
Sheet 12: Cluster_Analysis

Hierarchical and partitioning cluster analyses provide data-driven, unsupervised classification of specimens based purely on their geochemical similarities, without reference to pre-defined provenance groups. This approach serves as an independent validation of the group structure identified through geological interpretation and supervised methods (LDA, PCA). Three linkage methods for hierarchical clustering are applied — Ward's minimum variance, complete linkage, and average linkage — together with K-means partitioning at K=4, 5, and 6. Cluster validity indices (Silhouette, Davies-Bouldin, Calinski-Harabasz) assess the internal coherence and separation of the resulting cluster solutions.

Column Definitions
Column Name	Data Type	Description & Scientific Significance
Sample_ID	Text	Unique specimen identifier.
KMeans_K4	Text	The cluster label assigned to the specimen by K-means analysis with K=4 clusters. K=4 is examined to test whether the dominant geochemical variation in the dataset naturally resolves into four groups, potentially corresponding to major tectonic divisions of Anatolia. Cluster labels are arbitrarily assigned (C1–C4) and must be interpreted in geological context.
KMeans_K5	Text	The cluster label from K-means analysis with K=5 clusters, corresponding to the number of geologically defined provenance groups. K=5 is the primary K-means solution and its concordance with the geologically motivated group assignments validates the geological interpretation.
KMeans_K6	Text	The cluster label from K-means analysis with K=6 clusters, testing whether subdivision of one of the primary five groups is warranted by the data. Systematic splitting of a single group under K=6 may indicate the presence of compositional sub-populations within a provisionally unified provenance group.
Ward_Cluster	Text	The cluster assignment from hierarchical agglomerative clustering using Ward's minimum variance linkage criterion, which minimises the total within-cluster sum of squares at each merging step. Ward's method is generally considered the most appropriate for geochemical datasets as it produces compact, approximately spherical clusters that accord well with the assumption of multivariate normal group distributions.
Complete_Link_Cluster	Text	The cluster assignment from hierarchical clustering using the complete linkage (farthest-neighbour) criterion, which defines cluster distance as the maximum pairwise distance between members. Complete linkage tends to produce more compact clusters than single linkage and is sensitive to outliers, making it a useful cross-check on Ward's solution.
Average_Link_Cluster	Text	The cluster assignment from hierarchical clustering using average linkage (UPGMA), where cluster distance is defined as the mean of all pairwise distances. UPGMA is widely used in archaeometric ceramic studies and its results provide a third independent hierarchical clustering solution for comparison with Ward's and complete linkage results.
Silhouette_Score	Decimal	The Silhouette coefficient for the specimen's cluster assignment, ranging from −1 to +1. Silhouette scores measure how similar a specimen is to its own cluster (cohesion) compared to the next-nearest cluster (separation). Values above 0.7 indicate strong cluster membership; 0.5–0.7 reasonable membership; <0.5 suggests the specimen may be better placed in an adjacent cluster or that the cluster structure is weak.
Davies_Bouldin_Idx	Decimal	The Davies-Bouldin index contribution for this specimen's cluster, measuring the ratio of within-cluster scatter to between-cluster separation. Lower Davies-Bouldin values indicate better-defined, well-separated clusters. This index penalises cluster solutions with overlapping or highly elongated clusters and is particularly informative for assessing marginal specimens near cluster boundaries.
Calinski_Harabasz	Decimal	The Calinski-Harabasz index (Variance Ratio Criterion) contribution, measuring the ratio of between-cluster dispersion to within-cluster dispersion. Higher values indicate better cluster definition. This index increases monotonically with the number of clusters in K-means solutions and must be interpreted with caution; it is most useful for comparing solutions at the same K value.
Dist_to_Centroid	Decimal	The Euclidean distance of the specimen from its cluster centroid in standardised geochemical variable space. Specimens at large distances from their centroid are cluster periphery members that may represent transitional compositions or genuine outliers. Large distances in combination with low silhouette scores identify the most compositionally ambiguous specimens in the dataset.
Bootstrap_Support_%	Decimal	The percentage of bootstrap resamplings (typically n=1000) in which the specimen is grouped with the majority of its assigned cluster members, providing a measure of the stability of the cluster assignment under data perturbation. Bootstrap support >90% indicates a stable, reproducible cluster assignment; values <70% indicate assignments sensitive to the specific dataset composition.
Within_SSQ	Decimal	The within-cluster sum of squares contribution of the specimen, measuring the squared Euclidean distance from the specimen to its cluster centroid summed across all standardised variables. This metric is the minimisation criterion in K-means clustering and its specimen-level values identify the largest contributors to within-cluster heterogeneity.
Between_SSQ	Decimal	The between-cluster sum of squares contribution associated with the specimen's cluster, measuring the squared distance of the cluster centroid from the overall dataset centroid weighted by cluster size. The ratio of Between_SSQ to total SSQ (Within + Between) provides the R² of the cluster solution, measuring the proportion of total variation explained by cluster structure.
REE_Cluster_Match	Text	The cluster group label from cluster analysis applied exclusively to the REE concentration variables (Sheet 03), facilitating comparison of REE-only clustering with the full multi-variable clustering documented in the preceding columns.
Sr_Cluster_Match	Text	The cluster group label from cluster analysis applied exclusively to Sr isotope data (Sheet 05), enabling assessment of the concordance between isotope-based and composition-based cluster structures.
Petro_Cluster_Match	Text	The cluster group label from cluster analysis applied to the petrographic modal analysis data (Sheet 06), providing a textural/mineralogical cluster structure for comparison with the geochemical solutions.
Consensus_Cluster	Text	A consensus cluster label derived by majority voting across the six individual cluster solutions (K-means K4, K5, K6, Ward, complete, average) and the three analytical-domain-specific solutions (REE, Sr, petrographic). The consensus approach reduces sensitivity to the specific clustering algorithm and provides a more robust data-driven grouping.
Agreement_Score	Decimal	A numerical agreement score (0–1) representing the proportion of clustering solutions in which the specimen is grouped with the majority of its consensus cluster members. Perfect agreement (1.0) indicates the specimen is assigned to the same cluster by all methods; low scores (<0.6) indicate that the specimen's classification varies between clustering approaches and warrant focused investigation in the multi-proxy framework.
Provenance_Group	Text	The final multi-proxy provenance group assignment for comparison with the data-driven cluster assignments across multiple methods.

Sheet 19: Trace_Ratios_Anomalies

This sheet presents a suite of trace element ratios and REE anomaly parameters that provide geochemically specific provenance discriminants beyond the primary REE concentrations documented in Sheets 03 and 04. Inter-element ratios are particularly valuable because they are less sensitive to dilution by temper additions and post-depositional alteration than absolute concentrations: ratio values are preserved even when absolute concentrations are shifted by these secondary processes. The ratios selected here have established utility in geochemical provenance discrimination, drawing on trace element pair systematics (Zr/Hf, Nb/Ta, Y/Ho) that reflect specific mineralogical controls, and anomaly parameters (Ce/Ce*, Eu/Eu*, Gd/Gd*) that encode redox and fractionation histories.

Column Definitions
Column Name	Data Type	Description & Scientific Significance
Sample_ID	Text	Unique specimen identifier.
Ce_Ce*	Decimal	The cerium anomaly calculated from chondrite-normalised values as CeN/(0.5×LaN + 0.5×PrN). Values <0.90 indicate a negative Ce anomaly; values >1.10 a positive anomaly. Ce anomalies are sensitive to the redox history of the depositional environment of the clay source and to secondary oxidation during weathering and burial.
Eu_Eu*	Decimal	The europium anomaly EuN/√(SmN×GdN), the single most diagnostic REE ratio for distinguishing ceramics derived from granitic/felsic sources (Eu/Eu*<0.85) from those derived from more mafic, carbonate, or pelitic sources (Eu/Eu* closer to 1.0). Sm anomalies are generally absent in geological materials, and values significantly deviating from 1.0 may indicate analytical issues with Sm or Nd measurements rather than genuine geochemical anomalies.
La_Ce_ratio	Decimal	The La/Ce concentration ratio, typically ~0.45–0.50 in most crustal materials (reflecting the Oddo-Harkins regularity of even Ce over odd La). Values significantly different from this range may indicate fractionation of Ce from La through oxidative Ce⁴⁺ mobilisation or through differential mineral hosting of La versus Ce in the source rock.
Nd_Sm_ratio	Decimal	The Nd/Sm ratio, providing a normalisation-independent measure of LREE fractionation. Nd/Sm ratios in the range 4–6 are typical of upper continental crustal compositions; values outside this range indicate unusual LREE fractionation in the source lithology. The Nd/Sm ratio is a primary discriminant in Sm-Nd isotope geochemistry.
Tb_Yb_ratio	Decimal	The Tb/Yb ratio, measuring the gradient of the HREE pattern from mid-HREE (Tb) to late HREE (Yb). Steep gradients (high Tb/Yb) indicate garnet fractionation in the source rock, as garnet preferentially retains HREE over MREE. Flat HREE patterns (low Tb/Yb) indicate non-garnet bearing sources (amphibolites, granodiorites).
Ho_Yb_ratio	Decimal	The Ho/Yb ratio, a HREE fractionation indicator sensitive to the stability of garnet, amphibole, and zircon in the source rock. Ho/Yb values can discriminate between different metamorphic facies zones in potential clay source areas and complement the Tb/Yb ratio in characterising HREE pattern slopes.
Y_Ho_ratio	Decimal	The Y/Ho ratio, one of the most geochemically discriminating element pair ratios in provenance studies. Y and Ho have identical ionic radii and valence state (both 3+) and therefore an almost constant Y/Ho ratio of 26–28 in terrestrial igneous and clastic sedimentary materials. Deviations from this range (>35) indicate marine carbonate contributions, while values <25 suggest unusual hydrothermal or diagenetic processes.
Zr_Hf_ratio	Decimal	The Zr/Hf ratio, a geochemical pair that is nominally constant at ~36 in undifferentiated crustal materials (reflecting Goldschmidt's rule of twin elements). Deviations from this value indicate fractionation through magmatic differentiation, hydrothermal processes, or unusual mineral phases such as baddeleyite (high Zr/Hf) or hafnon (low Zr/Hf). Systematic Zr/Hf variations between provenance groups may reflect source rock differentiation levels.
Nb_Ta_ratio	Decimal	The Nb/Ta ratio, nominally ~17.5 in undepleted mantle and most continental crustal materials. Low Nb/Ta ratios (<10) may indicate arc-related volcanic sources with Nb depletion relative to Ta; elevated ratios (>20) may indicate continental crustal differentiation processes. The Nb/Ta ratio is among the most sensitive indicators of tectonic setting in geochemical provenance analyses.
Ba_La_ratio	Decimal	The Ba/La ratio, reflecting the relative enrichment of Ba (an element concentrated by subduction-related fluids) relative to La (concentrated in continental crust). High Ba/La ratios (>40) in ceramics may indicate clay sources in subduction-related volcanic arc settings, distinguishing Pontide ceramics from Central Anatolian basement-derived compositions.
Sr_Nd_ratio	Decimal	The Sr/Nd ratio, relevant to the Sr-Nd isotope systematics of the ceramic source material. In mantle-derived rocks, Sr/Nd~15; in continental crust, Sr/Nd varies widely depending on feldspar content. This ratio provides context for interpreting the ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr data in Sheet 05 in terms of source rock major-phase mineralogy.
Th_U_ratio	Decimal	The Th/U ratio, reflecting the degree of U mobility during weathering and diagenesis. The continental crust has a Th/U ratio of ~3.8–4.0. Values >5 may indicate U depletion through oxidative weathering of the clay source; values <3 may indicate U enrichment from organic-rich reducing environments. This ratio provides a secondary indicator of clay source depositional conditions.
Rb_Sr_ratio	Decimal	The elemental Rb/Sr ratio, controlling the rate of ⁸⁷Sr production in the Rb-Sr isotopic system. This ratio is reproduced from the Sr isotope sheet (Sheet 05) for convenience in combined trace element-isotope correlation analyses. Rb/Sr correlates positively with ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr across the five provenance groups.
Ba_Sr_ratio	Decimal	The Ba/Sr ratio, providing information on the relative partitioning of these two alkaline-earth elements between different feldspar and carbonate phases in the source rock. Ba/Sr >10 indicates Ba-enrichment relative to Sr, typical of K-feldspar-dominant and illite-rich compositions; values <3 indicate Sr-enrichment characteristic of plagioclase-rich or carbonate source rocks.
La_Nb_ratio	Decimal	The La/Nb ratio, discriminating between subduction-related (high La/Nb, reflecting Nb depletion in arc magmas) and within-plate or passive margin (low La/Nb) tectonic settings. This ratio effectively distinguishes ceramic clay sources derived from Pontide arc-related volcanics (high La/Nb) from those derived from within-plate Central Anatolian crystalline rocks (moderate La/Nb).
Hf_ppm	Decimal	Hafnium concentration in parts per million, the geochemical partner of Zr in the zircon mineral phase (ZrSiO₄ with Hf substituting for Zr). Hf concentrations are controlled primarily by zircon abundance in the source material and, together with Zr, define the Zr/Hf ratio documented in the preceding column.
Provenance_Group	Text	The assigned provenance group, enabling assessment of systematic inter-group differences in trace element ratios and anomaly parameters that complement the primary REE and major element discrimination documented in earlier sheets.

 
Sheet 20: MultiProxy_Framework

The Multi-Proxy Integration Framework constitutes the analytical synthesis of this dataset, bringing together the provenance assignments from all three primary analytical methods — REE fingerprinting, strontium isotope ratios, and petrographic analysis — together with the major element XRF data, to produce final, confidence-weighted ceramic provenance assignments. The multi-proxy approach is the central methodological contribution of this research framework: by requiring concordance between independent analytical methods, the probability of erroneous provenance assignments is substantially reduced relative to single-method approaches. This sheet documents the method-specific group assignments, inter-method concordance statistics, final provenance conclusions, distances to nearest clay sources, geological formation matching assessments, and the inferred trade route implications of each specimen's provenance assignment.

Column Definitions
Column Name	Data Type	Description & Scientific Significance
Sample_ID	Text	Unique specimen identifier as defined in the master Sample_Registry (Sheet 01), serving as the primary key linking the final provenance synthesis to all supporting analytical data across the 19 preceding sheets.
Site_Name	Text	The archaeological site name from which the specimen was recovered, reproduced in this summary sheet to facilitate direct reading of provenance assignments without requiring cross-referencing of Sheet 01.
Period	Text	The Bronze Age chronological period of the specimen, providing temporal context for interpreting the provenance assignment in relation to the known dynamics of Bronze Age Anatolian exchange networks, which varied significantly between the Early, Middle, and Late Bronze Age.
REE_Group	Text	The provenance group assignment derived specifically from the REE concentration fingerprinting analysis, as documented in Sheets 03, 04, and 10. This REE-based assignment reflects the geological source of the ceramic clay as indicated by its rare earth element pattern, independent of isotopic or mineralogical evidence.
Sr_Isotope_Group	Text	The provenance group assignment derived from strontium isotope analysis, as documented in Sheet 05. The Sr isotope-based assignment reflects the geological source of the ceramic clay as indicated by its ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr ratio and associated geochemical parameters, providing an isotopic provenance constraint independent of REE chemistry and petrography.
Petrographic_Group	Text	The provenance group assignment derived from petrographic thin-section modal analysis, as documented in Sheet 06. The petrographic assignment reflects the mineralogical and textural characteristics of the ceramic fabric that associate it with particular geological source formations, providing a physical-mineralogical provenance constraint independent of solution-based geochemical analyses.

REE-Sr concordance is the primary inter-method agreement indicator because these two methods employ fundamentally different analytical approaches (concentration versus isotope ratio) and therefore provide genuinely independent lines of evidence.
REE_Petro_Agree	Text	A YES/NO flag indicating whether the REE-based geochemical assignment and petrographic group assignment are concordant. REE-petrography concordance confirms that the geochemical signature and the mineralogical fabric of the ceramic are consistent with derivation from the same geological source formation.
Sr_Petro_Agree	Text	A YES/NO flag indicating concordance between the Sr isotope-based and petrographic provenance group assignments. Sr-petrography concordance provides additional confirmation of the multi-proxy provenance assignment, particularly valuable for specimens where REE data may be uncertain.
All_3_Agree	Text	A YES/NO flag indicating whether all three primary analytical methods (REE, Sr isotope, and petrography) yield concordant provenance group assignments. Three-way concordance is the highest standard of multi-proxy evidence and supports high-confidence provenance conclusions. Discordant three-way assignments require investigation of potential post-depositional alteration, mixed clay sources, or analytical errors before a final assignment is made.
Confidence_Level	Text	A qualitative confidence level for the final provenance assignment, classified as High (>90% probability, typically requiring all three methods to agree), Moderate (70–90%, typically requiring two of three methods to agree), Low (50–70%, based on only one method or borderline multi-method evidence), Ambiguous (<50%, conflicting evidence between methods), or Indeterminate (insufficient evidence for assignment). Confidence levels guide the weight assigned to each specimen in ceramic exchange network reconstructions.
Final_Provenance	Text	The final provenance group assignment for the specimen, representing the synthesis of all available analytical evidence. For specimens where all three methods agree, the final assignment is unambiguous. For specimens with inter-method discordance, the final assignment is determined by a weighted combination of method-specific confidence values, with highest weight given to REE fingerprinting and Sr isotope data over petrography.

Nearest_Clay_Source	Text	The identifier of the nearest clay source locality (from Sheet 14) whose geochemical composition matches the specimen's provenance group assignment. This field links the multi-proxy provenance synthesis to the geological reference dataset and provides the specific source locality that serves as the best available match for the ceramic's clay composition.
Geological_Formation_Match	Text	An assessment of the correspondence between the ceramic's geochemical and isotopic signature and the geological formation assigned to its provenance group, classified as Confirmed (multiple analytical methods independently identify the same formation), Probable (strong evidence from at least two methods), Possible (suggestive evidence from one method), Unlikely (geochemical evidence does not support the formation match), or Unknown (insufficient comparative data for assessment).
Trade_Route_Implied	Text	The inferred trade route or exchange mechanism implied by the combination of ceramic provenance group assignment and the location of the recovery site, drawn from the range of known or proposed Bronze Age Anatolian exchange routes: East-West Anatolia corridor, Pontic coastal route, Tauride mountain pass, Aegean seaborne, Mesopotamian overland, Trans-Caucasian, Local procurement, or Unknown. Trade route inferences must be treated as hypotheses requiring corroboration from independent archaeological evidence.
Interp_Category	Text	The interpretive category for the ceramic specimen based on its provenance assignment and recovery context: Local production (ceramic likely produced near the recovery site using local clay), Non-local import (ceramic produced at a distance and transported to the site), Regional exchange (ceramic produced and distributed within a regional interaction sphere), Long-distance trade (ceramic transported over distances >200 km), Workshop tradition (production technology indicates specialist workshop, irrespective of clay source location), Itinerant potter (possible production by travelling craftsperson using local clay at a non-home-base location), or Uncertain.
Quality_Score_100	Integer	A composite quality score on a 0–100 scale reflecting the overall analytical quality and evidential strength of the multi-proxy provenance assignment. The score integrates method concordance, individual method confidence, analytical QC flags from Sheets 15 and 16, sample preservation quality, and the reliability of the clay source comparator data. Scores above 80 indicate publication-ready, high-quality provenance assignments; scores below 50 indicate assignments requiring additional analytical work before incorporation into synthetic interpretations.
Notes	Text	Free-text supplementary notes on the multi-proxy provenance assignment, recording observations relevant to the interpretation of the final assignment, recommendations for additional analyses, references to relevant published parallels, or explanatory notes for discordant method assignments. Entries are drawn from a standardised note pool covering common scenarios encountered in multi-proxy ceramic provenance research.

 
Methodological Notes and Data Usage Guidelines
The present dataset has been constructed to reflect the full complexity of a genuine multi-proxy archaeometric study of Bronze Age Anatolian ceramic provenance. All geochemical values have been generated using group-specific parametric distributions calibrated to ranges reported in the published archaeometric literature for Anatolian Bronze Age ceramics, ensuring that the data are scientifically plausible and analytically realistic throughout.
Users of this dataset should note that Sample_ID values serve as the primary key for cross-sheet linkage. All analytical sheets reference specimens by their Sample_ID as defined in Sheet 01 (Sample_Registry). The five provenance groups (Group-A through Group-E) maintain internally consistent geochemical signatures across all analytical sheets, meaning that a specimen's major element composition, REE fingerprint, strontium isotope ratio, and petrographic characteristics are mutually coherent and consistent with its geological source designation.
The multi-proxy integration documented in Sheet 20 represents the analytical synthesis of all preceding data and constitutes the primary output of the methodological framework. The concordance statistics, confidence levels, and trade route interpretations in Sheet 20 should be interpreted in conjunction with the raw analytical data in Sheets 02–19 and the reference databases in Sheets 13 and 14.
For quality control purposes, users should consult the ICP-MS parameter documentation (Sheet 15) and reference standard QC monitoring data (Sheet 16) to assess the analytical reliability of the geochemical measurements. QC flags in Sheet 02 (XRF major elements) and the Outlier_Flag in Sheet 05 (Sr isotopes) identify specimens requiring additional scrutiny before inclusion in quantitative provenance interpretations.
